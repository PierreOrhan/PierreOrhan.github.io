## Information Geometry

The field of information geometry merges the tools of Differential Geometry and Information Theory (first built by Shannon). It presents a geometrical interpretation of probability distributions and explain how we can interpret statistical models with geometry. Questions that can be adressed with such tools are the following: What is the right gradient to use when optimising a neural network? What does it mean for a system to be optimal? <br>
Some researchers think it is a really attracting fields for theoretical Neuroscience. I am also trying to discover it. What is the promise? We discovered that in many parts of the brains, the firing of a population of cells is constrained on a manifold. To be more precise: the population activity vectors (a n-dimensional vector for a system of n-neurons, each dimension representing the firing rate of each neuron) are constrained on the manifold. One example is the head direction system which is constrained to a ring. So maybe there is a way to bridge a link between this manifold, and the ones that are considered in Information Geometry? Then we could use all the tools that were developed, at least theoretically. <br>

- A first good reading is the Mathematical Theory of Communication by Claude E. Shannon and Warren Weaver. It is really well explained and some examples are astounding. Shannon had in mind a framework built on channels and sources. I wonder if it is readily applicable to a mathematical theory on spaces that are a bit weird?
