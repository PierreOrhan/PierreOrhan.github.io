## BOOKS I LOVED

- 🔥 Neuronal dynamics. To me this was the perfect book to get a first insight into how neurons and their dynamics is modeled. While not being completely extensive on every subject, the book is perfectly organised and presented, a <a href="https://neuronaldynamics.epfl.ch/online/"> version is even available online for free (under copyright) </a>! 
- 🔥 The Neocortex. Some key personnalities across Neuroscience met to discuss the last 30 years finding on the Neocortex and provide research directions for the future. Separated in small groups, the researchers managed to deliver a concise reports on many aspects of the neocortex, making the book unvaluable. Nonetheless I feel that some author decided to include personal reflections which would not have made the round, yet they remain interesting and good readings. A version can be found <a href="https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/neocortex"> here </a>.
- The heart of the brain. Gareth Leng introduces the reader to the study of hormones. An amazing book with surprises at each page for the reader coming with a Mathematical or ML background as I was. <a href="https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/heart-brain"> Link </a>
- How we learn. Why Brains Learn Better Than Any Machine . . . for Now. Stanislas Dehaene introduces very accessible educational concept derived from Neuroscience. To me the "recycling hypothesis" he suggest is very interesting. Concisely: specific architectures and codes arose in specific part of the brain through evolution. For Stanislas Dehaene, these codes do not have to be solely used for their intial function. Instead the brain could benefit from their existence and slighty adapt them to perform new coding (recycling).
